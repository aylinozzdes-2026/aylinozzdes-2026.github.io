About

I am a university instructor and researcher at Clarkson University, specializing in psychology with a focus on human development, memory, and cognition. My work explores how people perceive, remember, and interpret their experiences, with particular interest in how perspective and context shape memory accuracy.

In the classroom, I am deeply committed to making complex psychological concepts engaging, relatable, and applicable to everyday life. I teach a range of topics including personality, human development, and core principles of psychology, and I strive to create an environment where students feel encouraged to think critically and connect theory to real-world behavior.

My research examines the mechanisms underlying memory distortion, including how visual perspective during encoding can influence later recall. Through this work, I aim to contribute to a better understanding of how memory functions in both everyday settings and applied contexts.

Originally from Turkey and now living in the United States with my family, I bring a multicultural perspective to both my teaching and research. Outside of academia, I enjoy cooking a variety of cuisines, exploring new ideas, and balancing professional life with family life.
